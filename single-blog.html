How can women justify their own beating?

Story Written by A. Martin

I will start with a question. What is the largest contributing factor to why, when women around the world were asked if it is justified that a husband beats his wife, do they say yes? Could it be the Education Rate, or perhaps the Nutrition and Access to Healthcare? What about GDP per capita, or is it simply an issue of culture and where in the world they live?

Before we go any further, let me be clear. I am not running an analysis on why men beat their wives and the ways in which this number can be reduced. That is far too large a question with almost no clear answer. Instead I wish to look at why women think its ok for the violence they endure, and is there a solution to lowering this figure.

To begin, lets talk about metrics. What data am I using and why.

The response variable - 'Justification' - is measured by the World Bank as 'Percentage of women ages 15-49 who believe a husband/partner is justified in hitting or beating his wife/partner for any of the following five reasons:'

  1. Argues with him

  2. Refuses to have sex

  3. Burns the food

  4. Goes out without telling him

  5. She neglects the children

This dataset is not perfect, nor is it complete. In fact it has data for just 102 countries, approximately half of all soverign states.

So immediately we know our analysis is probably not going to be able to draw universal conclusions, especially as a quick look through the data shows that this information is not collected on a yearly basis, and some countries have not responded in over two decades. I had to make some very harsh culls to this data to keep it relevant but at the same time usable. The set for the charts below includes just 51 countries.

Now, we turn to three of the explanatory variables I am using in this analysis: 'GDP per Capita', 'Nutritional and Basic Medical Care' and 'Education Rate'. Before I get into the specifics of these three, here is a scatter graph plotting these potential causes against 'Justification'.

EDU
NUT
GDP

One thing instantly pops out when plotting 'Education Rate' and 'Nutritional and Basic Medical Care'. As metrics - they are just awful. They do nothing to explain the disparity between why some populations think women beating is acceptable, and others not.

To be more precise, 'Education Rate' is defined by UNESCO as 'The ratio of children of official school age who are enrolled in school to the population of the corresponding official school age.' This dataset looks specifically at secondary level education, as access to this is more of an indicator (in this day and age) of the country of a whole.

'Nutritional and Basic Medical Care' is a more subjective metric, as it was one of the factors used in the Social Progress Index it itself is comprised of metrics such as undernourishment, child mortality and infectious diseases. A bit of a left field choice when looking at justification of violence against women but it does make for some interesting Exploratory Data Analysis.

So, intital thoughts? It would seem GDP per capita has a strong relationship with Justification. Could throwing money at the problem work?

Looking at the graph, it would seem there is a downward trend line, and if anything it is exponentially decreasing. There is a snag however, many Africa nations (in blue) are making a vertical path up the grath, and American and European nations (in red) are making a horizontal path down the bottom. What if it is actually culture and location that are creating this disparity, and not GDP?

So we turn to an old friend, a regression analysis, to see how much of the data can be explained by location, how much by GDP and are either of these statistically significant?
